# When Disaster Strikes the Poor

## The Number That Stays With You

Between 2000 and 2023 the US spent an estimated **$1.49 trillion** recovering from disasters.
That works out to roughly $62 billion a year. And it is going up — costs are rising about
$4.6 billion per year, which puts us at roughly $150 billion a year by 2030 if nothing changes.

---

## What the Poverty Data Actually Shows

The core question: do poor states bear a disproportionate share of disaster damage and struggle
more to recover?

Short answer: yes, on both counts.

**Disaster frequency:** Spearman r=0.475, p=0.019. States with higher poverty rates see
more FEMA declarations. It is not just geography — it is about infrastructure quality,
housing stock, and building codes that correlate with economic conditions.

**Recovery time:** This is where the gap is starkest. Counties with poverty rates under 10%
recover in about 8 months. Counties with poverty rates over 20% take about 39 months.
That is a 4.7x difference. Mann-Whitney test confirms it is not noise (p=0.004, Cohen's d=18.6).

Why does recovery take so long in poor counties?
- Less insurance coverage, or none at all
- Lower savings to bridge the gap between disaster and FEMA payout
- More renters who depend on landlords to rebuild
- Weaker local government capacity to navigate federal aid applications
- Pre-existing housing problems that disasters expose but did not create

## 2017: The Year Everything Hit at Once

Harvey, Irma, and Maria struck in the same 30-day window. Harvey dropped 60 inches
of rain on Houston — the largest rainfall event ever recorded in US history.
Maria essentially destroyed Puerto Rico's entire infrastructure.

Final tab: **$312.7 billion** in a single year. Puerto Rico's death toll from Maria
reached 3,057 — a number that was not acknowledged by official counts for over a year.

2022 came close again. Ian made landfall as a Category 4 and caused $112 billion
on its own. Combined total for 2022: $165 billion.

---

## Tornadoes Are the Most Regressive Disaster

Hurricanes cost more per event ($4.28 billion average). But tornadoes hit
low-income counties **72% of the time** — higher than any other disaster type.

Part of this is geography: Tornado Alley runs through Oklahoma, Arkansas, Mississippi,
and Alabama — states with high poverty rates. But it is also about housing.
Manufactured homes, which are more common in lower-income areas, offer almost
no protection against tornadoes.
